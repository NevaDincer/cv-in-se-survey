
## 1. Comprehension

Beginners tend to fixate on technical details, but careful reading goes beyond that.

Ask these 4 questions for every paper:

1. **What is the problem?** What gap or weakness does this paper target? Why does this work need to exist?
2. **What is new?** What does the paper contribute that wasn't there before — a method, algorithm, framework, evidence?
3. **How do they back it up?** What makes the claims scientific rather than just opinion — proofs, experiments, benchmarks, case studies?
4. **What do we take away?** Does this change how the field should operate? How far do the results generalize?

The abstract is designed to answer all four. Start there. Think of every paper as a story — these four questions are its plot structure.

---

## 2. Evaluation

Be fair, not just critical. A paper that looks obvious now may have been groundbreaking when published — always read in historical context.

For beginners: focus first on whether the claims are actually valid (methodology, proof correctness, experimental setup). Judging the significance of a problem requires broad field knowledge you may not have yet — but that shouldn't stop you from forming opinions.

Key checks:

- Is the problem worth solving, or is it artificial?
- Are the contributions genuinely new, or just incremental?
- Do the experiments actually support the claims? Are there confounding factors, unfair comparisons, overgeneralizations?

---

## 3. Synthesis

New ideas don't come from nowhere — engaging with existing research is one of the most effective ways to develop your own research directions.

Every paper is an opportunity to ask: what would I do differently? Some useful angles:

- Could the claims be supported in a different way?
- What is the strongest counterargument to the authors' case?
- Can the results be extended or applied elsewhere?
- What open problems does this work leave behind?

---

## 4. Paper Review Format

A review has three parts: **summary, critique, synthesis.**

- **Summary** — restate the paper in your own words: motivation, contribution, methodology, conclusion. If you can't paraphrase it, you don't understand it yet.
- **Critique** — pick 2–3 specific points of disagreement and argue them with technical reasoning. Don't just repeat the paper's own limitations section. Be specific, not generic.
- **Synthesis** — propose 1–2 original directions the work could be extended. Don't repeat the paper's future work section.

---

## 5. Professional Review

When a paper goes through peer review, referees follow essentially the same comprehension and evaluation process. The difference is they don't write a synthesis section. Volunteering to help your supervisor review papers is one of the best ways to internalize how academic publishing actually works.
